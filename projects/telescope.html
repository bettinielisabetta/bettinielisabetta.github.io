IRIS Testbed — Structural & Thermal Modeling

Engineering work on high-precision opto-mechanical systems, combining finite element modeling, experimental dynamic characterization, model validation, and lumped-parameter thermal analysis.
Organization NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL)
Program Habitable Worlds Observatory (HWO)
Focus Structural dynamics, FEM validation & thermal modeling

Problem & Context

High-precision optical systems are extremely sensitive to structural dynamics and thermal distortions. Resonant vibrations can amplify disturbances, degrade optical alignment, and limit control performance, while thermal gradients may introduce deformations that affect system stability.

Within the development activities for the Habitable Worlds Observatory, the IRIS testbed provides a ground-based platform for studying the structural and thermal behavior of precision opto-mechanical architectures.

Habitable Worlds Observatory (HWO) Concept of a next-generation space telescope designed to directly image and characterize Earth-like exoplanets. Source: NASA.

Objective

My work supports the development of a high-fidelity engineering representation of the telescope testbed, with the goal of characterizing its dynamic behavior, validating numerical models against experimental measurements, and extending the analysis toward thermal and thermo-structural effects.

Engineering Workflow

The project combines structural dynamics, experimental validation, and thermal modeling to support model updating, control-oriented analysis, and future thermo-structural coupling.

Structural FEM High-fidelity Abaqus model of the telescope assembly, including bench, support structures, optics, interfaces, boundary conditions, connectors, couplings, and isolator representations.

Modal Characterization Extraction of natural frequencies, mode shapes, modal participation factors, and modal effective mass through Lanczos-based modal analysis.

Experimental Dynamics Processing of accelerometer and hammer-test data to compute PSDs, FRFs, coherence, and frequency-domain indicators for dynamic validation.

FEM–Test Correlation Comparison between simulated modal behavior and experimental response to identify dominant modes, frequency shifts, and modeling uncertainties.

Lumped Thermal Model MATLAB-based node network representing steady-state heat transfer through conduction paths, contact conductances, and convection with the surrounding environment.

Model Reduction & Coupling Preparation of reduced-order structural models and thermo-structural coupling workflows to support control applications and optical stability assessment.

Technical Contributions

High-fidelity FEM model

Developed detailed structural models of the telescope assembly for modal characterization and dynamic response assessment.

Boundary condition modeling

Implemented realistic support conditions, isolator representations, connector elements, and coupling strategies to capture testbed-level dynamics.

Experimental data processing

Built MATLAB workflows for accelerometer and hammer-test data analysis, including PSD, FRF, and coherence estimation.

Model correlation framework

Compared numerical mode shapes and frequency-domain experimental results to support model validation and future model updating.

Lumped-parameter thermal model

Developed a node-based thermal network including conductive links, contact interfaces, convective terms, and steady-state thermal balance equations.

Control-oriented modeling

Supported the extraction and development of reduced-order models for downstream control and system-level analysis.

Thermal Modeling Approach

In parallel with the structural dynamics work, I developed a lumped-parameter thermal model of the telescope testbed in MATLAB. The model represents the system as a network of thermal nodes associated with the main structural and optical subsystems.

The thermal balance includes conductive heat transfer between connected components, contact conductances at mechanical interfaces, convection with the laboratory environment, and localized heat inputs from testbed equipment. The model provides a first-order estimate of the steady-state temperature distribution and creates the foundation for future transient and thermo-structural analyses.

Key Engineering Challenges

Capturing realistic boundary conditions and isolator behavior without over-constraining the FEM model.
Correlating numerical modal predictions with experimental PSD and FRF measurements.
Managing the trade-off between model fidelity, computational cost, and control-oriented simplification.
Representing uncertain thermal contact conductances and conductive paths between complex interfaces.
Extending separate structural and thermal models toward future thermo-structural coupling.

Next Steps

Ongoing work includes further FEM–experiment correlation, refinement of model fidelity, extraction of mass and stiffness matrices for reduced-order modeling, extension of the thermal model toward transient analysis, and thermo-structural coupling to assess the impact of temperature gradients on optical stability.
